A beautiful first solo destination

Taiwan felt like the perfect place for a first solo international trip: easy to move around, full of food, packed with character, and exciting without feeling overwhelming. It had city energy, mountain views, cozy cafés, and moments where getting slightly lost was part of the fun.

Itinerary

Day 0: Arrival at Taoyuan Airport

I arrived at Taoyuan Airport at around 3 AM, marking the start of my Taiwan trip. Although the airport is located quite far from Taipei city center, transportation was still convenient even at that hour, with buses available just outside the terminal. For my first night, I stayed at 📍Taipei Inn, a practical choice near Taipei Bus Station, which made the late-night arrival much easier.

The first moments felt surreal. I was nervous, excited, and very aware that I was doing this without the comfort of familiar people around me. But that was also what made it so special.

Day 1: Taipei 101, Sun Yat-Sen Memorial Hall, and Ximending Walking District

I checked into 📍Wow Hostel, which turned out to be an excellent budget stay. It is in the city center and near the metro which made the experience even better. My first stop was Sun Yat-Sen Memorial Hall, and although my iPhone Maps left me slightly confused along the way, spotting Taipei 101 in the distance immediately made it worthwhile.

After exploring the memorial hall, I made my way to Taipei 101 for a panoramic city view. As a budget traveler, I chose to visit Simple Kaffa on the 88th floor instead of paying for the full observatory, and it was a great alternative. In the evening, I headed to Ximending Walking District, where I enjoyed the lively atmosphere, street performances, food stalls, and countless shops. It was the perfect introduction to Taipei’s energy and charm.

Day 2: DIY Taichung, Miyahara, National Taiwan Museum of Comics, and Painted Animation Lane

On my second day, I headed to Taichung on my own, taking the Taiwan High Speed Rail from Taipei Main Station. I booked the one-way ticket through Klook for PHP 950, and the trip itself already felt like part of the adventure. My first stop was Miyahara, a famous ice cream shop known for its beautiful, Harry Potter-like interior. It was once a Japanese eye clinic built in 1927, which made the place feel even more unique.

After that, I spent the day simply wandering around Taichung. I visited the National Taiwan Museum of Comics, which was full of comic exhibits, archives, and creative spaces, then ended the day at Painted Animation Lane, a colorful alley filled with cartoon and anime murals. For someone who enjoys exploring at their own pace, Taichung felt fun, easy, and full of little surprises.

Day 3: Shifen, Shifen Waterfall, and Jiufen Old Street Night Tour

This was one of the highlights of my solo trip to Taiwan. To make things easier, I booked the tour through KKday and met the group at Taipei Main Station. Our first stop was Shifen, a charming old town known for its railway tracks, nostalgic streets, and sky lantern experience. I bought my lantern through Klook for PHP 430, and releasing it felt like such a special moment.

Next, we visited Shifen Waterfall, often called Taiwan’s Niagara Falls because of its wide and dramatic view. The final stop was Jiufen, which felt magical, especially at night. With its narrow alleyways, lantern-lit streets, free food tastings, and beautiful coastal view, it truly felt surreal. Many people say it looks like a scene from a Ghibli movie, and honestly, I could see why. As a budget traveler, I kept it simple and just enjoyed a cup of black tea at a café while taking in Jiufen’s dreamy atmosphere.

Day 4: The Red House, Bopiliao Historical Block, and Lungshan Temple

Another rainy day in Taiwan made me slow down and keep things simple. I started at The Red House, one of Taipei’s well-known cultural and commercial spots, before heading to Bopiliao Historical Block, a preserved street that beautifully shows off old red-brick architecture from the Qing Dynasty. It felt like a quiet step back in time and was an easy side trip since it was close to my next stop.

I ended the day at Lungshan Temple, one of the most famous temples in Taipei. Dedicated mainly to Guanyin, the Goddess of Mercy, along with other folk deities, it had a calm and spiritual atmosphere that felt even more special with the light rain outside. It was a simple day, but one that gave me a deeper look into Taipei’s history and culture.

Day 5: Beitou Hot Spring Museum, Thermal Valley, and Private Hot Spring

As the rain kept pouring, I looked for something to do near Ximending and decided to visit Beitou Hot Springs. It turned out to be such a good choice, especially for a convenient half-day trip from Taipei. Easily accessible by MRT, Beitou offers a mix of sulfuric hot springs, scenic views, and a bit of history, along with photogenic spots like Geothermal Valley.

What made it even more memorable was that it was my first time experiencing a real private hot spring. I honestly found it so cool and relaxing. Between the peaceful atmosphere and the unique experience, Beitou felt like the perfect escape from the rainy city.

Day 6: Capybara Knight

As this was my last day in Taiwan, I spent some time buying pasalubong for myself and for my family back in the Philippines. After that, I went to Capybara Knight, a charming capybara café in Taipei. It was such a cute and relaxing way to end my trip, especially after days of exploring the city.

The café gave me the chance to interact with adorable capybaras, along with other animals like rabbits, ducks, and a cat. The highlight was the feeding session, which also came with a drink and a capybara-shaped snack. Seeing how gentle and friendly the capybaras were made the experience even more special, and it felt like the perfect peaceful ending to my Taiwan adventure.

Things to know

Things to know before visiting Taiwan

Before sharing my itinerary and tips for places to visit and things to do, let me share a few things that you should know before you go. To be completely frank, I did not know too much about Taiwan before planning my first trip. I just knew Taiwan because of F4 and Taipei 101.

So, here is what I learned and think is important to know before a trip.

⛅ Weather

I went to Taiwan in October, and honestly, the weather was quite unpredictable. A typhoon had just passed before I even left the Philippines, so by the time I arrived, it was still hot, rainy, and a little chaotic. In my opinion, this is not the best time to visit, especially if you want a smoother and more comfortable trip.

For travelers who prefer less heat and lower typhoon risk, I would recommend going around late November to February, especially for a Taipei city trip. Just keep in mind that northern Taiwan can still feel damp during winter. Still, compared to October, the weather is generally more manageable, making it a better time for sightseeing and slow travel.

🥢 Food

As an Asian traveling to another Asian country, I did not find it too hard to adjust to the food in Taiwan. In fact, it felt comforting in many ways, especially if you already enjoy Chinese or Taiwanese cuisine. If you love flavors like soy sauce, pork, noodles, rice meals, and dumplings, then Taiwan will definitely not disappoint.

One thing I really loved was how easy it was to enjoy good food even on a budget. From 7-Eleven meals to local restaurants and night market snacks, there was always something tasty to try. I found myself enjoying simple meals like onigiri, dumplings, and warm rice dishes while walking around the city. Even though not every famous dish matched my taste, the overall food experience in Taiwan was still one of the highlights of my trip. Just be prepared because some restaurants do not have English-translated menus.

💸 Bring Cash

One tip I would really recommend is to bring cash in Taiwan, especially for smaller purchases during the day. Major credit cards are accepted in many tourist-oriented businesses and larger establishments, but having New Taiwan Dollars with you makes the trip much easier.

♨️ Hot Springs

Hot springs in Taiwan are a must-try, especially because of the island’s volcanic geology. I was still in awe when I visited Xin Beitou and experienced both a private hot spring and a public hot spring for the first time. It felt so relaxing and unique, and it easily became one of the most memorable parts of my trip.

What I also found interesting is that, unlike in Japan, you can still enjoy many hot springs in Taiwan even if you have tattoos. That makes the experience feel more accessible and less intimidating for travelers who might usually worry about that.

🛕 Temples

Temples in Taiwan are not just beautiful places to visit, they are deeply part of everyday life. For travelers, visiting a temple in Taiwan is not only about architecture, but also about understanding the country’s beliefs and traditions. A good example is Longshan Temple in Taipei.

💁‍♀️ No tipping

One thing I found convenient in Taiwan is that there is generally no strong tipping culture, so you do not have to worry about adding extra money after every meal or service. In many hotels and restaurants, a 10% service charge is already included in the bill, which makes things simpler for travelers.

For me, this made traveling around Taiwan feel more relaxed and budget-friendly. I could just enjoy the food, cafés, and local experiences without overthinking tips, which is especially helpful when you are traveling solo and trying to keep things simple.
What to do and avoid

Taiwan dos & don’ts for solo travelers

Do

Use the metro often. It makes getting around Taipei easy and less stressful.
Carry cash for small food stalls, markets, and simpler local spots.
Stay flexible. Some of your best moments will happen when plans change.
Bring an umbrella or light rain jacket because weather can shift fast.
Try local food even when you do not fully know what it is yet.

Don’t

Do not overpack your days. Taiwan is better when you leave room to wander.
Do not rely only on cards everywhere. Some places still work better with cash.
Do not panic if you get a little lost. That is part of the solo travel experience.
Do not skip hostel social moments if you want to meet people naturally.
Do not treat rainy weather like a ruined day. In Taiwan it can add to the mood.
